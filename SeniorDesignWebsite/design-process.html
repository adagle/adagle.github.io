Leg Design & Dynamics Calculations

​Paco has 6 C-shaped fiberglass legs, which can be modeled as mass-less linear springs that bend under compression. By measuring the compressed length of the leg under the load-bearing force (weight of the robot), it is possible to derive a spring constant. Then, the dimensions of the robot can be used to calculate the force and torque on Paco's center of mass as a function of angular position.

Leg Dimensions and Stress Analysis

​The robot legs are nearly semicircles with a slight extension on both ends for the attachment and foot contact point. The sizes of the legs were carefully determined and tested in simulations in accordance with the following diagram:
SolidWorks was used to determine a preliminary spring constant, k, of the legs as well as the change in length corresponding to the compression length of the spring system. Built in SolidWorks Simulation tools were used to measure the stresses and deformation of the leg subject to a load of 10 lbf, approximately 1/3 the weight of the robot. This simplification assumes that each leg will support approximately an equal weight of the robot as the robot walks in an alternating tripod gait, meaning 3 legs should be in contact with the wheel at any given time.

For simulation purposes, the leg was fixed at the point where it attaches to the leg clamp and hinges to the leg shafts extending from the motors. The load modeled is the ground normal force pointing upwards, supporting the weight of the body on the leg. Computational analysis results indicated k is approximately 10308.74 N/m and compressed length is 4.315 mm. These results are shown in the figure below. Notably, Fiberglass is a custom made material with properties that vary depending on the fiberglass sheets and resin used. Therefore, Solidworks provides an imperfect model for measuring material properties and merely serves as a guide. These results could be determined more accurately by applying a 10 lbf load to an individual leg, measuring the compression along the diameter axis and using a spring force equation to derive a corresponding k value.

The body force in the x, y and z components can then be calculated as a function of the angular position of each leg,  spring force, and ground reaction force. The sum of these forces for each leg then represents the force vector applies to the body in the body frame B. 
By specifying the robot dimensions, it is then possible to calculate the x,y,z components of the torque and force on the body relative to the body frame. The cross product of the location matrices for each leg and the force on the body yields the torque on the body. This is equivalent to taking the skew symmetric of the leg position matrices multiplied by the force matrix.

Literature Review

Literature Search

Our literature search focused on optimizing our design by gaining a better understanding of design parameters used by previous RHex robots or similar mechanisms. Our search was divided into three main parts: internet search, journal search, and patent search. 
​

​During our initial internet search, we found that the RHex was originally funded by a 1998 DARPA project that lasted five years. Several prototypes were built with different goals in mind such as walking, jumping, and running. Most of these simple behaviors were achieved using open loop controls while more advanced functions such as line following and bipedal walking required feedback from sensors and on-board cameras [1]. Boston Dynamics designed their own rugged RHex robot which incorporated inverted operation, carried up to 2 kg of payload and featured a teleoperation camera [2]. The University of Pennsylvania's Kod Lab created several variations of the RHex, incorporating modular payload systems and pushing the limit of agility for outdoor rough terrain usage [3]. 

In each different design, the overall form of the robot was mostly standard; each robot had six legs controlled by six independent actuators which were controlled by an on-board computer. The leg designs varied between robots, and therefore prompted a more thorough search for the journal and patent sections. 
​
​Most of the journal research was done to examine the legs of the RHex robot, which are critical to the locomotion of this machine. Compliant legs were used on the latest variations of the RHex robots. These are legs that use the stored elastic potential energy from compression to help the robot spring forward with each step. The journal entry, Reliable Stair Climbing in the simple hexapod ‘RHex’, provides an in depth comparison of three leg designs. The compass, four bar, and half circle leg each provide unique capabilities to the hexapedal robot, but ultimately the journal concludes that the half circle leg increases stability by increasing the amount of contact points between the leg and terrain [4].
​Another journal article discusses how to identify various terrain based on the torque that each leg experiences. The torque is measured using statistical analysis of the current going to each independent motor. A threshold value for current must be provided to serve as a comparison to other potential terrains [5].   

The journal article, Modeling of a Hexapod Robot; Kinematic Equivalence to a Unicycle, was used as our primary source to conduct a dynamic analysis. The journal article simplifies each leg to a spring, and allows for the calculation of forces and torque to be executed using MATLAB [6].
​We started our search with Martin Buehler's patent for a hexapedal robot. Buehler is a leader in dynamic locomotion robotic technology and is one of the primary inventors of the hexapedal (RHex) robot. His patent describes the use of six separate actuators that individually control each leg to achieve a tripod gate. We then used this patent to do a category search on relevant designs including “compliant leg”, “hexapedal robot” and “multipedes” [7]. 
​    Patent US 7249640 B2 describes a mobile robot capable of running and jumping on rough terrains. Its unique use of “WHEGS”, a hybrid of wheels and legs, allows for climbing and running at sustained speeds over 10 body lengths per second. This robot also uses an alternating gait similar to the one employed in our design [8].
    
    Patent US 8789630 B2 outlines the design of a variable stiffness compliant leg for a hexapedal robot. The legs change length to adjust the elasticity and allows the vehicle to adapt to different terrains. Based on the patent, we found that most compliant legs are made out of a composite with high tensile strength such as Delrin or Fiberglass [9].
